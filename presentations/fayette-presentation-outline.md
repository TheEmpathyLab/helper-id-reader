# Look for the Helpers — Then Be One
**A 45–60 minute interactive session for Fayette Senior Services**  
*Presented by Shelton Davis, Empathy Lab / Helper-ID*

---

## Session Overview

This session uses emergency preparedness as a doorway into a larger conversation about community resilience — how we ask for help, how we offer it, and how active older adults are often the most underutilized resource in their own neighborhoods. Participants leave with two completed cards, one new skill awareness, and at least one new connection in the room.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of the session, participants will:
- Have completed a personal emergency information card they can carry and store at home
- Have named at least one practical skill or resource they can offer their community
- Have identified one unmet need in their own life they are willing to ask for help with
- Understand the landscape of senior services and community gaps in Fayette County

---

## Agenda

### Welcome & Framing *(5 minutes)*
Brief introduction, one laugh, and a simple question to the room: *"When was the last time someone showed up for you when you really needed it?"* Short reflection — no pressure to share. Sets the emotional tone without manufacturing vulnerability.

Frame the session arc: **We are going to talk about three things. How to get help. How to give help. And what our community still needs.**

---

### Ring 1 — How to Get Help *(15 minutes)*

**The honest question:** What would happen if something happened to you right now — today — and the people around you didn't know who to call, what medications you take, or whether you have a pacemaker?

**Interactive moment:** Before any statistics, ask the room: *"Who in this room has their emergency information written down and accessible to someone other than themselves?"* Show of hands. No judgment — just reality.

**Brief stat layer:** Share 2–3 grounding statistics on medical emergencies, first responder response gaps, and the role of bystander information in outcomes.

**The tool:** Participants complete a **Helper-ID emergency information card** — a simple, structured form capturing medical conditions, medications, allergies, emergency contacts, insurance, and blood type. Every participant leaves with a wallet card and a full-size copy for home, housed in a Helper-ID envelope.

*This is the preparedness floor — the minimum viable version of being findable and knowable in a crisis.*

---

### Ring 2 — How to Give Help *(15 minutes)*

**The pivot:** *"Now that yours is done — who in your life doesn't have one of these yet?"*

Transition to a harder and more interesting question: **We talk a lot about being prepared to receive help. We talk less about being prepared to give it.**

**Interactive moment — the skills inventory:** Ask the room to call out practical things they know how to do. Facilitate a whiteboard or verbal list. Fix a leaky faucet. Deliver a meal. Give a ride to a medical appointment. Explain a Medicare statement. Sit with someone who is grieving. Help a neighbor fill out a form.

*The point: you do not need a credential to be a Helper. You need availability and willingness.*

**The "I need help with" card:** Each participant writes down one real, current, small thing they need help with — something they haven't asked anyone to do. It doesn't get collected or shared without consent. But it gets written. Because naming it is the first step to asking.

**Brief reflection:** We stay silent about small needs. We go to Facebook. We hope someone notices. And often the simple tasks remain undone — not because no one would help, but because we never asked. Being prepared isn't just for emergencies. It includes knowing how to ask for help on a Tuesday.

---

### Ring 3 — What Our Community Still Needs *(10 minutes)*

**Zooming out:** What does Fayette County look like for the people in this room — and for the people who aren't here?

**Brief grounding:** Share what is actually happening locally around senior services, transportation gaps, social isolation, and the decline of "third places" — the community spaces where people used to bump into each other naturally. Loneliness among older adults is a documented health risk equivalent to smoking 15 cigarettes a day. This is not abstract.

**The activation question:** *"Given what you just put on that skills card — where do you think you could show up?"* Not a commitment. A possibility.

Brief overview of volunteer pathways, neighbor-to-neighbor programs, and Fayette Senior Services' own community needs. The goal is not to recruit — it is to help participants see themselves as assets, not just beneficiaries.

---

### Closing — You Are Already a Helper *(5–10 minutes including Q&A)*

Bring it back to the opening question. Someone showed up for you once. Probably more than once. They didn't have a degree in it. They just showed up.

**The closing line:** *"Play is not just for kids. And being a Helper doesn't require a degree."*

**The silly task — and the real one:**

Each participant shares their favorite animal and the noise it makes. They are instructed: if you see someone from this room out in the world — at the grocery store, at the doctor's office, anywhere — you may ask them: *"What was your animal?"* They must say it and make the noise.

Why? Because we forget names but we remember faces. And because being a Helper starts with being willing to be a little ridiculous in service of connection.

## What Participants Leave With

- Completed Helper-ID emergency card (wallet size + full size, in envelope)
- "I need help with" card (personal, not collected)
- A mental skills inventory — one thing they can offer
- At least one new face they know by animal
- A light understanding of Fayette County's community landscape and where they might fit

---

## What I Bring

- Printed card forms and envelopes for all participants
- A short slide deck (visuals only — no bullet points read aloud)
- A portable printer for on-the-spot card production if feasible, or pre-printed forms
- Whiteboard markers or large sticky notes for the skills inventory exercise
- Myself — no staff support needed, no setup beyond a table and a room

---

## A Note on Helper-ID

Helper-ID is the tool used in Ring 1 of this session to produce the emergency information card. It is one example of how this kind of information can be captured and stored. Participants are encouraged to use whatever format works best for them — this card, a handwritten sheet on the fridge, a note in their phone. The goal is the information existing somewhere accessible. Helper-ID is the vehicle for today's exercise, not the point of the session.
